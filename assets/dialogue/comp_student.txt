Computer Science Student:
Okay, look — I know everyone’s got their own opinion about operating systems, but honestly, once you’ve used Linux long enough, you just get it. Like, Windows feels like being in a padded room where someone hands you crayons and tells you it’s “for your own safety.” macOS? Beautiful, yes, but it’s like living in a glass house — polished, stylish, and completely useless when you want to actually do something. But Linux? Linux is freedom. Linux is chaos. Linux is enlightenment through suffering.
Now, I’ve tried them all — Ubuntu, Fedora, Pop!_OS, even that weird one my friend swears by that uses i3 and doesn’t even have a desktop environment by default (yeah, he’s that guy). But nothing compares to Arch Linux.
Arch isn’t just an operating system. It’s a lifestyle. It’s like learning to cook instead of ordering takeout — sure, you could get something fast, but where’s the satisfaction? Installing Arch teaches you more about your machine than any textbook ever could. You don’t just install it — you become it.
When you boot into your minimal command line for the first time after installing Arch, it’s just you and the void. A black screen. A blinking cursor. Infinite potential. You build everything yourself — the desktop environment, the window manager, even your font rendering. Every pacman -S feels like laying a brick in the cathedral of your own creation. And when something breaks — and it will break — you don’t panic. You learn. You Google. You read man pages like they’re sacred scripture. You scroll through Stack Overflow like an archeologist digging through layers of ancient wisdom. And when you fix it? The dopamine hit is unreal.
People ask me why I love it so much, and honestly, it’s because Arch doesn’t hold your hand. It gives you a kernel, a package manager, and a dream. The rest is up to you. It’s like Linux’s way of saying, “I believe in you.”
And the Arch Wiki — oh my god, the Arch Wiki is the closest thing to divine knowledge we have on this earth. I swear it could teach you how to fix a broken relationship if you formatted it correctly. Any problem you have — driver issues, weird kernel panic, GPU meltdown — the Wiki has the answer. And if it doesn’t, some 14-year-old on Reddit probably does.
But I get it — Arch isn’t for everyone. Sometimes you just want something that “works out of the box.” That’s cool. That’s fine. Fedora is great if you want to live on the bleeding edge without too much blood loss. Pop!_OS is fantastic if you want Ubuntu with personality. But for me? Arch is where I belong. It’s honest. It’s unforgiving. It’s beautiful.
People think Linux users are smug because we say “btw, I use Arch.” But that’s not smugness — that’s trauma bonding. We’ve all stared into the abyss of a bootloader issue at 3 a.m. and emerged stronger. We’ve all typed sudo rm -rf / at least once in a moment of blind confidence. We’ve earned our stripes, one broken kernel at a time.
I still remember the first time I configured my i3 window manager. It was bare, minimal, clean — just me and my terminal. No distractions. No bloat. I spent two hours just setting keybindings and felt like a god. Then I spent another three trying to fix my audio, which had mysteriously stopped working after an update. But that’s the beauty of it! Every problem is a puzzle. Every fix, a small victory.
So yeah — maybe Linux isn’t for everyone. Maybe some people like pretty GUIs and easy installers. That’s fine. But me? I like to suffer a little for my art. I like knowing exactly what every process on my system is doing. I like building my setup from scratch and watching it boot up faster than my friends’ overpriced MacBooks.
Arch Linux isn’t perfect. But it’s mine. Every config file, every alias, every .bashrc line — they all have a story. And that’s something no commercial OS can give you.
